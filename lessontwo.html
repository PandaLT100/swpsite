Dialogue


Marco: ¡Maria! Hola.

Maria: Hola. ¿Cómo estás?

Marco: Bien. Eh….. (He hesitates) ¿Cómo se dice “I eat an apple.” en español?*

Maria: “Yo como una manzana”

Marco: ¡Gracias! (He runs away and Maria looks confused)


* ¿Cómo se dice __ en español? - How do you say __ in Spanish?



Subject Pronouns


Yo - I

Tú - You [Casual]

Él - He, him

Ella - She, her

Usted - You [Formal]

Nosotros - We, us

Ustedes - You(guys) [Plural]

Ellos - They, them [Masculine]

Ellas - They, them [Feminine]



The Present Tense


Spanish has 3 types of verbs: -Er verbs, -Ar verbs, and -Ir verbs

-Er verbs are verbs that end in “-er”, -Ar verbs are verbs that end in “-ar”, and -Ir verbs are verbs that end in “-ir”. No exceptions.

What is conjugation? Conjugation is the action of changing a verb according to the tense of the sentence and the subject.

Ex: In English, “To like” changes to “Like” when following “I” (I like) and it changes to “Likes” when following “She” (She likes) in present tense sentences.

To conjugate any verb to about any tense in Spanish, you would need to take away the -er, -ar, or -ir ending and add the new ending according to the subject and tense of the sentence.

To Conjugate -Er And -Ir Verbs

Pronoun Ending Pronoun Ending
Yo -o Nosotros -emos (er), -imos (-ir)
Tú -es Ustedes -en
Él/Ella/Usted -e Ellos/Ellas -en

Ex: Yo como (I eat)

Yo - I

Comer (to eat) → Com-(er) → Com-(o) → Como


To Conjugate -Ar Verbs

Pronoun Ending Pronoun Ending
Yo -o Nosotros -amos
Tú -as Ustedes -an
Él/Ella/Usted -a Ellos/Ellas -an

Ex: Ella habla (She speaks)

Ella - She

Hablar (to speak/to talk) → Habl-(ar) → Habl-(a) → Habla


Note: Note that when your sentences include names, or something similar that would make it hard for you to choose a conjugation ending, try to match the name with its proper pronoun before choosing the ending.

Ex: Bob eats an apple. → Bob is a “he”. You can easily replace “Bob” with “He”. (He eats an apple). So, you’re going to use the ending for “He”, which is “-e” → Bob come una manzana.


Definite And Indefinite Articles


Definite Articles

El - The (Masculine)

La - The (Feminine)

Los - The (Masculine, Plural)

Las - The (Feminine, Plural)

Ex: La manzana (The apple)


Indefinite Article

Un - A/An (Masculine)

Una - A/An (Feminine)

Unos - A/An, Some, A few (Masculine, Plural)

Unas - A/An, Some, A few (Feminine, Plural)



Nouns


Spanish has masculine and feminine nouns. This does not mean that things are boys or girls. This just means that nouns are grammatically feminine and masculine.

To tell the difference between a masculine and feminine noun, you can look at the letter the word ends in.

If the word ends with 'o', 'n', 'r', 's' or 'pa', it is usually masculine.

Ex: Libro (Book)

If the word ends in 'a', 'e', 'cion', 'sion', 'dad', 'ed', etc., it is usually feminine.

Ex: Caja (Box)

NOTE: There are exceptions (Like “agua” [water]) to masculine or feminine nouns. If you are struggling to figure out whether a noun is masculine or feminine, don't be afraid to look it up or ask someone.

If you would like to turn nouns into their plural forms, all you would need to do is add “s” or “es”.

If the noun ends in a vowel, add “s” to the end of the word.

Ex: Manzana (Apple) → Manzanas (Apples)


If the noun ends in a consonant (not including “x” or “s”), add “es” to the end of the word.

Ex: Elevador (Elevator) → Elevadores (Elevators)

Note: If that noun ends in “n” and happens to have a tilde, turning it into its plural form will remove the tilde.

Ex: Canción (Song) → Canciones (Songs)

If you would like to add something like an article or adjective to indicate or describe a noun, that adjective or article (or whatever you're using) MUST match the noun in question. For example, if the noun is masculine singular, then your article must also be masculine singular. If the noun is feminine plural, then your adjective must also be feminine plural.

Ex: Los carro - The car ❌ (“Los” is masculine plural. “Carro” is masculine singular. It does not match.)

El carro - The car ✔ (“El” and “carro” are both masculine singular. They match!)



Making Simple Sentences


In order to to make simple sentences, Spanish has the something called SVO. Although, this is only the basic structure of a sentence.

“SVO” stands for 'Subject' (What/Who you are talking about), 'Verb' (The activity being done [such as eating]), and 'Object' (What is being affected by the verb in the sentence).

Ex: Yo como una manzana. (I eat an apple.)

“Yo” is your subject. Who or What are we talking about? Me!

Note: If your subject is obvious, you can remove it completely from your sentence.

“Como” is your verb. What are we doing? Eating!

“Una manzana” is your object.
